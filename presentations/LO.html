History of the National Lawyers Guild

  • Founded in 1937 as the nation’s first racially integrated bar association
  • Involved in a number of landmark cases and activities integral in building the grassroots movements necessary to protect civil liberties and human rights
  • Examples:
    • (1940s) Prosecuted Nazis at Nuremburg
    • (1945) Guild chosen by US Gov’t to officially represent the American People at the founding of the United Nations
    • (1960s) Represented families of civil rights activists murdered by local law enforcement/Ku Klux Klan members
    • (1960s/70s) Represented Vietnam War draft resisters
    • (1980s) Used international law to support anti-nuclear movement
    • (1990s) Defended anti-globalization, environmental, and labor rights activists
    • (2000s) Sent delegation to Gaza to document Israeli war crimes during 2008-09 invasion
    • Today . . . Continues in its roll to organize lawyers, activists, and community leaders to protect civil liberties and advance human rights

History of the Legal Observer (LO) Program

  • Established in 1968
  • Designed to enable people to express their political views as fully as possible without unconstitutional disruption
  • Part of a comprehensive system of legal support designed to enable people to express their political views as fully as possible without unconstitutional disruption

Who Are Legal Observers?

  • Typically, but not exclusively, law students, legal workers, and lawyers who may or may not be licensed locally
  • Open to everyone willing to be trained and respect the free space created by the program
  • The Legal Observe Program is not open to those working for or with government or law enforcement agencies
  • Attend public demonstrations as independent third parties
  • Observe and record incidents and the activities of law enforcement in relation to the demonstrators
  • Serve as a deterrent to unconstitutional behavior by law enforcement during a demonstration

When have LOs been useful recently?

  • Ferguson, MO: LO testified at a hearing that resulted in a preliminary injunction being filed against the use of a five second rule by law
  • Chicago, IL: LOs provided depositions in a civil suit for false arrest and testified in criminal trials related to the 2012 NATO demonstrations
  • Chicago, IL: LOs served as plaintiffs in a class action stemming from mass arrests at 2003 Iraq protests (named plaintiff: arrested LO attorney)
  • Bay Area, CA: LOs served as plaintiffs in a federal civil rights class action resulting from mass arrests during 2010 Oscar Grant protests (named

Confidentiality & Legal Concerns

  • Confidentiality Concerns:
    • Assume that all information obtained while serving as a LO is confidential
    • Immediately inform the LO coordinator & attorney(s) if you receive a subpoena/ other legal process related to your work as an LO
  • Legal Considerations:
    • LO’s are not “arrest-proof” –all dispersal orders and laws apply
    • Law enforcement is going to do what law enforcement is going to do, whether it’s legal/constitutional or not
    • Recording law enforcement is legal
    • This does not mean that law enforcement will not threaten to arrest you!
  • Again, do not document demonstrators engaging in anything that could be illegal and DEFINITELY do not share on social media or media.

Preparing for the Action

  • Some flexibility is required. This is guidance.
  • Prior to the Day of Action
    • Legal Observer Coordinator will contact you and other legal observers through app
    • Make arrangements to receive Legal Observer gear –green hat, note pads, pens, and sharpies, portable charges, snacks, etc.
    • Determine time/ place meet up location
  • Day of Action:
    • Check in with LO Coordinator, may be present or offsite
    • Locate other LOs and your LO “buddy”
    • Divide tasks and spread out

Preparing for the Role of Legal Observer (Equipment)

  • Notebooks and pens
  • Green Guild Legal Observer hat
  • Contact list
  • ID Card and Bar Card (if you have one)
  • Cell phone with full battery
  • Area map (if needed)
  • Police misconduct forms
  • Police Department phone numbers: precinct-division-HQ
  • Audio/video recorder, i.e. phone

Additional Equipment during Pandemic

  • Masks
    • If you can bring a couple. If tear gas is used, you will want to change your mask
  • Googles
  • Hand Sanitizer

Preparing for the Role of Legal Observer—Issue Spotting

  • Familiarize yourself with “Know Your Rights” Materials
    • www.nlg.org
    • www.civil-rights.net
  • Familiarize yourself with cases on police misconduct and civil rights violations specific to your jurisdiction
  • Familiarize yourself with potential defenses of protestors
    • E.g., justification, First Amendment defenses, de minimis infractions, self defense, defense of others, etc.
  • Familiarize yourself with the Charlotte Code
  • Familiarize yourself with potential charges arrestees might face
  • Familiarize yourself with potentially unconstitutional police tactics
    • E.g., “Pop-up” Police Lines, Denial of Access to Public Fora, Intimidating Displays of Force

Legal Observer Placement

  • Buddy System
    • If photograph or video recording is requested or becomes necessary, only one LO per pair should be documenting while the other keeps their eyes on the full context of the situation
  • For Large Demonstrations
    • Pairs in front, back, and middle
    • Floaters
  • For Small/ Stationary Demonstrations
    • Front, back, and sides

Take Notes What to Document Initially:

  • Law enforcement agency presence
    • City, County, State, Federal, Private security, etc.
    • Collect names and badge numbers, especially of those of higher ranks
    • Chain of command: who appears to be in charge?
  • Vehicles
    • Squad cars, unmarked vehicles, busses, wagons, motorcycles, bicycles, horses, etc.
  • Weaponry & Other Equipment
    • LRADs, flexi-cuffs, batons, tasers, tear gas, riot gear, etc.
  • Location/ Route of Demonstrators & Crowd Size
    • Presence of any Counter-Demonstrators
  • Always document location/ time of any

Documenting Arrests What to document if arrests occur:

  • Method/ manner of arrest, including use of force/ weapons used
  • Injuries
  • Arresting law enforcement agency and individual officer
  • Warnings given prior to arrest
    • Number
    • Method (verbal, recording, bull horn, etc)
    • Loud/Clear
    • Wording
      • Alternatives to arrest –dispersal/ new location

GET NAMES!

  • We need birth dates and names, if possible

Law Enforcement Behaviors to Document:

  • Things that significantly impact travel/ behavior:
    • Pop-up Lines
      • Misdirecting demonstrators, splitting demonstrators up, or detaining/arresting segments.
  • Kettling
    • Police lines that prevent people from joining or leaving a demonstration
  • Denial of Access to Public Forums
    • Police lines that prevent people from accessing public parks/ side walks
  • Displays of Force
    • Banging on riot shields; aiming pepper spray; thumping batons; verbal threats

Interacting with Police

  • Audio/Visual Recording Considerations:
    • Paper should be your primary source of documentation.
    • Smartphones:
      • Turn off cloud back-up
      • Enable your phone’s password
  • Date and time stamp everything!
  • Wide-angle shots are often more useful than close-ups
  • Record short “snippet” videos rather than one long one
  • Try to record audio during arrests

Audio/Visual Reminders

  • Try to never record demonstrators engaging in behavior that could be construed as illegal
  • Never record demonstrators who don’t want to be recorded. If someone asks not to be recorded, try to honor that request
  • Do not post to social media or share with anyone besides the legal team or legal observer coordinator

Social Media Dont’s:

  • Please do NOT post any notes or accounts of what you witness while Legal Observing to social media, including videos.
  • This is necessary in order to maintain the integrity of legally protected LO notes.
  • LO notes and observations can be extremely damaging to post publicly and it is most especially dangerous for Los to livestream on any social media.
  • Publicity is not a general function of Legal Observing.

Identifying Police (Infiltrators):

  • Assume there are cops present as “protestors.”
  • “Snitch-jacketing”
  • Counter-Protestors

Scenario #1: Planned Civil Disobedience

  • Try to get names and D.O.B. ahead of time
  • Contact an LO Coordinator immediately if they are not already aware
  • Coordinate with leaders/arrestees
  • Count total number of arrestees
  • Try to find out where they are being taken and any charges

Scenario #2: Mass Arrest

Mass arrests can be confusing and scary. If you see signs of mass arrest looming:

  • You are not useful as a Legal Observer if you get arrested.
  • Before effecting a mass arrest, cops must give at least one warning to tell the protesters how long they have to disperse before they will be arrested.
  • Write down the time you hear this warning, what exactly is said, how audible it is, how it is given (voice, bullhorn, etc) and any other orders.
  • If you don’thear this kind of warning, write that down too.

Scenario #2 cont’d: Mass Arrests

Signs of mass arrest –in general:

  • Flexicuffs
  • Pop-up Lines
    • Misdirecting demonstrators, splitting demonstrators up, or detaining/arresting segments.
  • Kettling
    • Police lines that prevents people from joining or leaving a demonstration
  • Police from other agencies suddenly appear in large numbers
  • Empty buses arrive with no route on the from banner
  • Special note: If a protest turns violent, expect mass arrests.

After the Action

  • Check back in with the LO coordinator and/or demonstration organizers
  • It’s important to let someone know you are leaving
  • If the demonstration was uneventful
  • If arrests or unconstitutional activity

What Legal Observers Do NOT Do

  • Engage in crowd control
  • Speak to the Media
  • Interfere with an arrest in progress
  • Provoke action
  • Negotiate with police on behalf of protestors
  • Provide direct legal advice to protestors or engage in the unauthorized practice of law
  • Get arrested! (Hopefully)

Legal Observation

  • Chapters, groups, and coordinators adapt to the situation and appreciate your flexibility.
  • Generally,
    • Organizations request observers (with as much notice as possible) through contact
      • That contact may be you
    • Organization designates contact person/alternate contact person, includes date/time of protest, nature of protest, expected attendance, any special circumstances
    • Coordinator is assigned
    • Coordinator or other Legal Observer puts out a call for LOs and gets team in communication with one another, often through an app like Signal
    • Team prepares for observation
    • Team conducts observation
    • Team maintains notes in a confidential manner or hands notes and any evidence over to Record Keeper/Coordinator
    • Team debriefs w/ Coordinaton
    • Coordinator follows up with Guild
  • Pay attention to special instructions for an observation.

Method: Legal Observing Basics Recap

  • Dress and Actions
  • Visibility

  • Objectivity
  • Interacting with Police

Dress and Actions

  • Avoid wearing clothes or buttons with political slogans
  • Dress comfortably but conservatively (wear comfortable shoes)
  • Maintain a calm but assertive demeanor
  • Act professionally
  • *Remember to bring special protection during the pandemic

Visibility

  • Your goal is to be visible to both activists and police
  • Wear identifying clothing (Legal Observer hats)
  • Announce presence to activists and note identifying clothing
  • Maintain central presence, if possible

Teamwork

  • Legal Observers are more effective in teams
  • If possible, work in teams of two
    • Scribe (takes notes, asks questions, points out dangerous conditions to Recorder)
    • Recorder (records action through audiotape, still camera, or video camera)
  • Check in frequently with Coordinator, especially if working by yourself

Objectivity

  • Think about what kind of person would best put the police on notice that their actions are being documented as part of a professional operation that will hold them accountable in Court for any violations of protesters’ rights
  • Attorneys who might rely on your notes need you to be thorough and professional
  • Credibility
  • Avoid blurring the line between protestor and activist
  • It might be helpful to explain to protestors what your role as a Legal Observer is

Interacting with Police

  • Do not argue with police
  • Do not fraternize with police
  • Remain calm and professional, but speak with a loud voice if necessary
  • Ask pointed questions
    • E.g.,
      • “Why are you asking protestors to leave this part of the park?”
      • “Where are you taking this person?”
  • Do not push police to the point of being arrested

On the Day

  • Show up a little before you’re supposed to be there
  • Check in with your Coordinator
  • Receive your location assignment and make sure you know where it is
  • Become familiar with any mass transit in the area, or arrange for other transportation
    • A common question is where you should park—we might be able to offer a suggestion but really that’s up to you
  • Exchange phone numbers
  • Check in with your Coordinator throughout the demonstration

During the Demonstration

Take detailed notes

  • What law enforcement agencies are present
  • Names, badge numbers, physical descriptions, especially of arresting officers
  • Who is in charge
  • Warnings given, who gave them, what they said
  • Routes taken by demonstrators (streets, times)
  • What media is present
  • Names of people arrested, conduct
  • Officers conduct and special circumstances

During the Demonstration (Continued)

  • Stay vigilant
    • Undercover police
    • Civil disobedience
    • Instigators
    • Counter-protestors
    • Hate groups
  • Avoid talking to the Media
  • Avoid becoming a go-between

If People are Arrested

  • Ask police where person is being taken
  • If directed to, go to appropriate precinct and talk to desk Sergeant, ask about charges and when protestors will be released
  • Get arrestee’s name, names and phone numbers for surrounding witnesses
  • Deal with addresses and statements later if needed
  • Make sure legal team, attorneys on call, legal aid, somebody knows about arrest

At the End of the Day

Check in with your Coordinator to

    • Sign out
    • Find out if you’re needed to go to a precinct or convergence center
    • Reflect on your notes, fill in any missing information, hand over any notes or evidence you’ve collected to the Record Keeper
